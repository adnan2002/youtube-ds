



# **Presentation Standards** 

## **Cover Page** 

- A cover page with the **project title, your name, and cohort** . 

- Give a brief introduction on your topic. You don’t need to have an ‘introduction’ slide. 





### **Problem Statement** 

- Make it **specific** : clearly define **what problem** you are addressing. 

- Frame your approach as **questions** or steps you are taking to solve the problem. ● Avoid paragraphs; keep it **concise** and clear. 



### **Key Metrics** 

- Total number of transactions/calls/customers/employees 

- Number of listings (if relevant, e.g., Airbnb, e-commerce) 

- Number of stations, trips (for transport datasets) 

### **Time Coverage & Location** 

- State the **years or period** the data covers (e.g., 2010–2020) and the **location** it’s set in.

## **Defining Key Terms (only if applicable)** 

**Explain terms** that will frequently come up during your presentation (E.g. Geographical aspects, Types of hackings). Give a brief overview to your audience. 



Using **Charts** to showcase your analytical findings. 

### **When to use Which Chart ?** 

Use the guide **‘Visual Vocabulary’** from Google Classroom to choose the right chart type based on your data and analysis goals. It helps you match your data's purpose whether it's comparison, trend analysis, distribution with the most effective visualization method. 

### **1. Slide Background for Charts** 

- A slide with a chart should only have the chart. 

- The chart must fill the slide: no extra shapes, images, or decorations. 

- The chart background color should match the slide’s background for a clean look. 

### **2. Slide Heading or Chart Title** 

- Only keep **one line of text at the top** : either the slide title _or_ the chart title. 

- Avoid repeating words or adding extra text. 

### **3. X and Y Axis Titles** 

- Keep axis titles **only if the values are not self-explanatory** . 

   - Example: If the x-axis shows “2016, 2017, 2018,” you don’t need to add “Years” as a title. 

- Use axis titles only when clarification is necessary. 

### **4. Key Findings / Text Boxes** 

- Do **not** add text boxes on the same slide as the chart. 

- A chart should stand alone and tell the story visually. 

- If extra explanation is needed, place it as bullet points on the **next slide** after the chart. 



2 



### **5. Data Labels** 

- If axes are shown → **no need for data labels** . 

- If axes are hidden → **keep data labels** to ensure values are visible. 

### **6. Numbers  Formatting** 

- Format numbers so they’re easy to read: 

   - Percentages → show as % 

   - Large numbers → use K (thousands), M (millions), B (billions) ○ Round numbers when appropriate to avoid clutter. 

### **7. Font Size** 

- Ensure values are visible to the entire audience (even from the back). 

- Recommended minimum font size: **14 pt** for charts. 

### **8. Colors** 

- Use a **consistent color theme** across all charts. 

- Ensure **high contrast** (e.g., avoid putting two dark colors together). ● Make sure colors are readable and don’t overwhelm the chart ● Keep **legends** with charts if necessary. 



- Conclusion in **bullet points** based on your findings. 





#### **Be Data-Driven** 

- Every recommendation should **directly come from your analysis** . 

- Use insights, trends, or patterns observed in your charts and findings as the basis. 



3 



#### **Avoid Speculation** 

- Do **not** include recommendations that aren’t supported by the data. 

- Avoid assumptions or personal opinions unless explicitly stated as hypotheses. 







- **Next Steps** to outline what could be done after your project, such as further analysis, implementation, or areas for improvement.
