### **Problem Statement vs Objectives in Data Analytics  Projects: A Hospital Case Study** 

In a data project, the **Problem Statement** and the **Objectives** play critical roles in defining the _scope_ and _purpose_ for analysis. This document outlines their differences and includes examples of good vs. bad problem statements using a hospital scenario. 

# **<mark>1. What Is a Problem Statement?</mark>** 

In its simplest form, a **problem statement** defines the pain point you hope to solve or the  impact you hope to make with your work or the opportunities that the project seeks to  address. A problem statement is articulated at the **beginning of a project** to set the stage  for analysis. 

A problem statement provides **context** and explains **why the project exists** . 

### **Characteristics of a Good Problem Statement:** 

- Specific and descriptive. 

- Clear and concise. 

- Focuses on the core issue affecting the organization. 

- Sets the foundation for analysis without diving into specific questions. 

### **What needs to be Mentioned in a Problem Statement:** 

- The _specific problem_ 

- A _numerical value_ to represent the magnitude of the problem 

- Location _(if available)_ 

- Timeframe _(if available)_ 

- _Impact_ of the problem (Why is it a problem? Were there any bad 

- consequences because of it?) 

### **Bad vs. Good Problem Statements** 

##### **_Example 1:_** _Increasing ER Wait Times_ 

###### **Bad Problem Statement** : 

"ER wait times are long." 

###### **Why it’s bad** : 

- It’s too vague, lacks context, and doesn’t describe the scope or impact of the issue. 

###### **Good Problem Statement** : 

" **Hospital X** has reported a **25% increase** in emergency room (ER) wait times over the **last  year** , leading to **a rise in patient complaints** and **a decline in satisfaction scores.** " 

###### **Why it’s good** : 

- Clearly defines the issue using a numeric value ( _25% increase in ER wait_ 

- _times_ ). • Provides context, including location and timeframe ( _Hospital X, last year,…_ ). • Lists the impact of the issue ( _rise in complaints and satisfaction decline_ ). 

#### **_Example 2:_** _Declining Patient Satisfaction_ 

###### **Bad Problem Statement** : 

"Patient satisfaction scores are low." 

###### **Why it’s bad** : 

- It doesn’t specify how low, over what period, or what might be causing the decline. 

###### **Good Problem Statement** : 

"Over the **past six months** , **Hospital Y** ’s **patient satisfaction scores have dropped** 

by **15%** ,  with survey feedback indicating dissatisfaction with **long wait times for specialist  appointments** . This decline in satisfaction is contributing to an **increase in patient  complaints** and a **potential loss of returning patients** ." **Why it’s good** : 

- Clearly defines and quantifies the issue ( _15% drop in satisfaction_ ).  • Provides 

context ( _Hospital Y, past six months_ , _dissatisfaction with specialist wait times,…)._ 

- Lists the impact of the issue ( _increase in patient complaints, loss of returning patients_ ). 

# **<mark>2. What Are Objectives/Approaches?</mark>** 

**Objectives** break the problem into specific, actionable queries that guide the data analysis.  They focus on **what needs to be analyzed and measured** to solve the problem. 

### **Characteristics of Good Objectives:** 

- Specific, measurable, and actionable. 

- Directly address aspects of the problem statement. 

- Help identify insights and guide decision-making. 

### **Bad vs. Good Objectives** 

#### **_Example 1:_** _ER Wait Times_ 

###### **Bad Objective** : 

"Why are wait times bad?" 

###### **Why it’s bad:** 

- It’s vague and subjective. 

- It doesn’t focus on any specific metrics or variables. 

**Good Objective** : 

"What is the **average ER wait time** over the **last 12 months** , and how does it vary **by day of  the week** ?" 

###### **Why it’s good** : 

- Focuses on measurable metrics ( _average wait time_ ). 

- Includes specific dimensions for analysis ( _day of the week_ ). 

#### **_Example 2:_** _Patient Satisfaction_ 

###### **Bad Objective** : 

"How can we make patients happier?" 

###### **Why it’s bad** : 

- It’s too broad and subjective, with no clear direction for analysis. 

###### **Good Objective** : 

"What **percentage of specialist appointments** had **wait times exceeding 30 minutes** in  the **past six months** , and how do these wait times **correlate with patient satisfaction  scores** ?" 

###### **Why it’s good** : 

- Focuses on measurable metrics ( _percentage of long waits_ ). 

- Explores relationships ( _correlation with satisfaction scores_ ). 

### **3. Key Takeaways** 

- A **problem statement** sets the high-level direction for a data analytics project by  explaining the **why** behind the analysis. 

- **Objectives** focus on specific, actionable queries that guide the **how** of the 

- analysis. • A **bad problem statement or objective** is vague, broad, and lacks 

focus, while a **good one** is clear, measurable, and actionable. 

### **Tips for Writing Problem Statements and Objectives** 

###### **1. Keep the Problem Statement Specific:** 

- Avoid diving into analysis specifics. 

- Set the scene and define the overall challenge. 

###### **2. Make Objectives SMART:** 

▪ **Specific** : Focus on a _single issue_ or metric. 

- **Measurable** : Use _quantifiable metrics_ . 

▪ **Attainable** : Ensure the question can be answered with _available_ 

_data_ .  ▪ **Reproducible** : Define _clear terms_ for consistency. 

- **Time-bound** : Specify a _timeframe_ .
